Malignant spinal cord compression›
StructuralHigh

Epidural tumour compressing cord or cauda equina — new/worsening back pain, leg weakness, sensory level, or bladder/bowel change in a cancer patient. Ambulant function is the clock: treat before it is lost.

Act now
  1. Dexamethasone 16 mg (oral or equivalent IV) as soon as MSCC is suspected — with PPI cover; do not wait for imaging.
  2. Whole-spine MRI within 24 h (CT with contrast if MRI contraindicated) — image the whole spine, lesions are often multilevel.
  3. Flat bed rest / spinal precautions until stability is assessed.
  4. Refer same-day to oncology + spinal surgery / radiation oncology; assess ambulatory status and document it (it drives prognosis).

Recognise

Suspect early. Back pain (often the first symptom, may precede neurology by weeks; worse on lying, straining, or at night), progressing to limb weakness, a sensory level, gait disturbance, and — later — urinary retention / incontinence and reduced anal tone. Thoracic spine is the commonest site.

Speed of onset matters. A motor deficit evolving over <24–48 h carries a worse prognosis and demands faster diagnosis than a deficit creeping over >7 days. Autonomic (bladder/bowel) signs are late — do not wait for them.

Commonest primaries: breast, prostate, lung, myeloma, renal.

Confirm

Whole-spine MRI within 24 h of suspected MSCC is the investigation of choice; whole-spine CT with contrast if MRI is contraindicated or unavailable. Image the entire spine — cord compression is multilevel in roughly a third of cases, and the plan changes if there is a second site.

Establish tissue diagnosis where the primary is unknown, but never let the biopsy delay steroids or definitive treatment in a threatened cord.

Manage

  • Corticosteroid: dexamethasone 16 mg (oral or equivalent parenteral) as soon as MSCC is suspected, then 16 mg daily while awaiting surgery or radiotherapy, with gastric protection and glucose monitoring; taper once definitive treatment is under way. Stop it if imaging excludes MSCC. (A 10 mg IV loading bolus is common practice but is not part of the NICE regimen.)
  • Definitive treatment — surgery vs radiotherapy: decompressive surgery followed by radiotherapy preserved and restored walking better than radiotherapy alone in the Patchell RCT (able to walk after treatment 84% vs 57%; among those non-ambulant at entry, regained walking 62% vs 19%). Favour surgery for a single area of compression, spinal instability, an unknown primary needing tissue, radioresistant histology, or deficit despite/after prior RT — in patients fit for it with a reasonable prognosis.
  • Radiotherapy for those not surgical candidates, radiosensitive tumours, multilevel disease, or poor performance status.
  • Systemic therapy can be first-line for exquisitely chemo/hormone-sensitive disease (e.g. lymphoma, germ cell, some prostate) alongside the above.
  • Bladder care (catheterise retention), VTE prophylaxis, pressure-area and bowel care, analgesia.

Disposition & escalation

Admit; discuss same-day with spinal surgery and radiation oncology (many centres run an MSCC coordinator / pathway). Pre-treatment ambulatory status is the strongest predictor of walking afterwards — patients treated while still walking usually stay walking; once paraplegic, few recover. This is what makes the 24 h window real. Definitive treatment ideally within 24 h of the diagnosis in a threatened cord.

Pitfalls

  • Waiting for bladder/bowel signs — by then the cord is often lost.
  • Imaging only the symptomatic level and missing higher compression.
  • Withholding steroids pending MRI, or omitting gastric/glucose cover.
  • Referring the day after, not the hour of, suspicion.

Superior vena cava obstruction›
StructuralHighYale 0–4

Obstruction of SVC flow — facial/neck/arm swelling, distended neck and chest-wall veins, dyspnoea, plethora; commonly lung cancer or lymphoma. Urgency is set by airway and cerebral signs, not the cosmetic swelling.

Act now
  1. Assess for the red flags — stridor / airway compromise, or cerebral signs (confusion, reduced consciousness, visual change from laryngeal/cerebral oedema). These make it an emergency.
  2. Sit the patient up; give oxygen.
  3. Get tissue first unless airway/cerebral compromise forces the hand — the treatment depends entirely on the histology.
  4. Endovascular stenting for severe or life-threatening (Yale grade 3–4) obstruction — fastest relief, and it does not need a diagnosis.

Recognise

Facial and neck swelling, dyspnoea and a sensation of fullness, distended neck and chest-wall collateral veins, facial plethora, cough; worse on bending forward or lying flat. Onset is usually gradual (collaterals develop), so most presentations are urgent rather than immediately life-threatening.

The dangerous minority: stridor or airway oedema, and cerebral oedema (headache, confusion, obtundation) — a rapidly occluding SVC that has outrun its collaterals. These override the usual biopsy-first rule.

Malignant causes dominate — non-small-cell and small-cell lung cancer, non-Hodgkin lymphoma; also consider catheter/device-associated thrombosis.

Confirm

Contrast CT of the thorax defines the level, extent and cause of obstruction and guides both biopsy and stenting. Secure a histological diagnosis (sputum/pleural cytology, nodal or bronchoscopic biopsy, image-guided core) before treatment — management is entirely disease-specific, and treating blind can forfeit a curable-intent diagnosis (e.g. lymphoma, small-cell, germ cell).

Manage

  • Endovascular stenting gives the most rapid symptom relief and is the treatment of choice for severe or life-threatening SVCO; it is independent of histology, so it can precede the diagnosis when symptoms are urgent.
  • Tumour-directed therapy by histology: chemotherapy (± the stent) for chemosensitive disease — small-cell lung cancer, lymphoma, germ cell; radiotherapy for non-small-cell lung cancer and other less chemosensitive tumours.
  • Anticoagulation if the obstruction is thrombotic or a stent is placed (per local protocol).
  • Corticosteroids: often given, but the evidence is weak and benefit uncertain outside steroid-responsive tumours (lymphoma) or to limit a radiotherapy-associated oedema flare — not a substitute for definitive treatment.
  • Head elevation and oxygen are supportive only.

Grade — Yale (Yu) system

  • G0 radiographic obstruction, asymptomatic.
  • G1 mild — head/neck oedema, plethora, cyanosis.
  • G2 moderate — oedema with functional impairment (mild dysphagia, cough, mild visual disturbance).
  • G3 severe — cerebral oedema (mild headache, dizziness) or laryngeal oedema, or diminished cardiac reserve (syncope on bending).
  • G4 life-threatening — significant cerebral oedema (confusion, obtundation) or laryngeal oedema (stridor), or significant haemodynamic compromise. G5 fatal.

Grade 3–4 is the threshold that pushes urgent stenting ahead of the biopsy.

Disposition & escalation

Grade 3–4 (airway or cerebral signs, haemodynamic compromise) → monitored bed, urgent interventional radiology for stenting, anaesthetic/ITU input for a threatened airway. Grade 1–2 → expedited work-up to tissue diagnosis then disease-specific treatment; genuine emergencies are the minority.

Pitfalls

  • Empirical radiotherapy before biopsy — it can render a lymphoma or small-cell unclassifiable and lose curative-intent treatment.
  • Treating SVCO as uniformly life-threatening: most is urgent, not peri-arrest — let the airway/cerebral signs, not the facial swelling, set the pace.
  • Missing catheter-associated thrombus as the cause.

SourcesYu JB, Wilson LD, Detterbeck FC, J Thorac Oncol 2008;3:811–814 (Yale/Yu classification & management algorithm; PMID 18670297). ESMO Handbook ch. 3.

Cardiac tamponade / malignant pericardial effusion›
StructuralPeri-arrest

Pericardial fluid raising intrapericardial pressure and choking cardiac filling — obstructive shock. Dyspnoea, tachycardia, hypotension, raised JVP in a cancer patient; can arrest as PEA.

Act now
  1. Echocardiography now — it makes the diagnosis (effusion + diastolic RV/RA collapse, IVC plethora) and guides drainage.
  2. Recognise decompensation: hypotension, tachycardia, raised JVP (Beck's triad), pulsus paradoxus (inspiratory SBP fall >10 mmHg).
  3. Urgent (echo-guided) pericardiocentesis for haemodynamic compromise — the definitive move; do not delay for a full oncological work-up.
  4. Bridge with IV fluids while preparing to drain; avoid intubation / positive-pressure ventilation if possible — it can precipitate arrest in a tamponade.

Recognise

Progressive dyspnoea, orthopnoea, fatigue, chest discomfort; on examination tachycardia, hypotension, raised JVP, quiet heart sounds and pulsus paradoxus. Beck's triad (hypotension, muffled sounds, distended neck veins) is specific but insensitive — the full triad is uncommon and its absence does not exclude tamponade. Tamponade is a physiological diagnosis: it is the haemodynamic effect, not the effusion size, that defines it — a rapidly accumulating small effusion can tampon where a large chronic one does not.

Common in advanced lung and breast cancer, lymphoma, melanoma; also radiation pericarditis and, rarely, immunotherapy-related.

Confirm

Echocardiography is the single most useful test: it confirms the effusion, sizes it, and shows the tamponade physiology (diastolic collapse of the right atrium then right ventricle, a plethoric non-collapsing IVC, respirophasic transvalvular flow variation). Effusion size by echo in diastole: mild <10 mm, moderate 10–20 mm, large >20 mm. ECG may show low voltages and electrical alternans; chest X-ray a globular heart. Send drained fluid for cytology.

Manage

  • Pericardiocentesis (echo-guided, or fluoroscopic) is indicated for tamponade and for symptomatic large effusions or suspected neoplastic aetiology; it is both diagnostic and therapeutic. Leave a catheter for extended drainage — it markedly cuts re-accumulation versus a single tap.
  • Temporising while you set up: IV fluid to support preload; vasopressor if needed. Avoid positive-pressure ventilation where you can — it drops venous return and can tip a compensated tamponade into arrest.
  • Prevent recurrence: extended catheter drainage, or a surgical pericardial window (subxiphoid or thoracoscopic) for recurrent/loculated effusions; intrapericardial sclerosing/cytotoxic agents are an option but cause chest pain and constriction and are used selectively.
  • Treat the cancer: systemic antineoplastic therapy for the underlying malignancy is the durable control.

Disposition & escalation

Monitored bed / HDU–ICU; involve cardiology and cardiothoracic surgery. Recurrence guides the escalation ladder: after isolated pericardiocentesis re-accumulation is common (~38% in pooled series), versus roughly 10–12% with extended catheter drainage, sclerosis or balloon pericardiotomy, and under ~7% after a surgical window — so a recurrent malignant effusion is a window/extended-drainage problem, not a repeat-tap one.

Pitfalls

  • Anchoring on effusion size — a small, fast effusion can tampon; a large slow one may not.
  • Intubating a hypotensive tamponade before drainage — risk of periarrest.
  • Aggressive diuresis / vasodilators — they cut preload and worsen the obstruction.
  • Single tap and discharge in malignant disease — plan for re-accumulation.

Raised intracranial pressure & brain-metastasis crises›
StructuralPeri-arrest

Mass effect / vasogenic oedema (or haemorrhage) from brain metastases raising ICP — headache, vomiting, drowsiness, focal deficit, seizure; herniation is the peri-arrest end.

Act now
  1. Recognise herniation: falling GCS, new pupillary asymmetry / fixed dilated pupil, Cushing response (hypertension + bradycardia), abnormal posturing — a neurosurgical emergency.
  2. Airway/breathing first; nurse head-up ~30°, head midline; treat hypoxia and hypercapnia.
  3. Dexamethasone for vasogenic oedema — up to 16 mg/day for severe raised-ICP symptoms (4–8 mg/day suffices for milder mass-effect symptoms); with PPI cover.
  4. For impending herniation: hyperosmolar therapy — mannitol 0.25–1 g/kg IV over 15–30 min, or 3% hypertonic saline — and call neurosurgery now.

Recognise

Raised-ICP syndrome: headache (worse on waking, coughing, lying flat), nausea and vomiting, progressive drowsiness, papilloedema, and evolving focal deficits. Seizures may be the presentation. Herniation red flags — declining consciousness, a new dilated/unreactive pupil, the Cushing response (hypertension with bradycardia), and extensor posturing — mark decompensation and demand immediate action.

Sudden deterioration suggests intratumoural haemorrhage (melanoma, renal, choriocarcinoma, thyroid) or obstructive hydrocephalus.

Confirm

Urgent CT head (with contrast, or MRI where feasible) for the number, size and site of metastases, midline shift, hydrocephalus and haemorrhage. Do not let imaging delay hyperosmolar therapy or the neurosurgical call in a herniating patient — treat and scan in parallel.

Manage

  • Corticosteroid (vasogenic oedema): dexamethasone — 4–8 mg/day for mild-to-moderate mass-effect symptoms, up to 16 mg/day for severe or raised-ICP symptoms; higher doses add toxicity without extra benefit in less-severe disease, so reserve them. Steroids are not indicated for asymptomatic metastases. Give gastric protection and monitor glucose; taper to the minimum effective dose. (A 10 mg IV loading bolus is common practice but is not a fixed guideline figure.)
  • Hyperosmolar therapy (herniation / critical ICP): mannitol 0.25–1 g/kg IV over 15–30 min, or 3% hypertonic saline as an alternative — a bridge to definitive decompression, not a treatment on its own.
  • Seizures: treat active seizures (benzodiazepine → load an antiepileptic); do not give prophylactic antiepileptics to seizure-free patients with brain metastases — routine prophylaxis is not recommended.
  • Definitive control: urgent neurosurgery (resection of a dominant/haemorrhagic lesion; CSF diversion for hydrocephalus) and/or radiotherapy (SRS for limited disease, whole-brain in selected cases); systemic therapy where the disease is exquisitely responsive.
  • Airway protection, head-up positioning, normocapnia; brief controlled hyperventilation only as a short bridge to theatre.

Disposition & escalation

Herniation or critically raised ICP → immediate neurosurgical referral, ICU/HDU, airway management. Stabilise oedema with steroids and decide definitive local therapy (surgery/RT) with neuro-oncology and radiation oncology, weighted to performance status, systemic disease burden and prognosis.

Pitfalls

  • Prophylactic antiepileptics in a seizure-free patient — no benefit, real interactions and toxicity.
  • Reflex high-dose 16 mg dexamethasone for every patient — match the dose to symptom severity; over-steroiding causes myopathy, hyperglycaemia, infection.
  • Relying on mannitol/hyperventilation as anything more than a bridge to surgical decompression.
  • Missing intratumoural haemorrhage or hydrocephalus as the cause of sudden deterioration.

Malignant central airway obstruction / stridor›
StructuralPeri-arrest

Tumour narrowing the trachea or main bronchi (intraluminal or extrinsic) → positional dyspnoea, stridor, monophonic wheeze; a compensated airway can close abruptly.

Act now
  1. Sit the patient up; high-flow humidified O₂; heliox (helium–oxygen) to ease flow through the stenosis while definitive treatment is arranged.
  2. High-dose corticosteroid for peritumoural oedema; nebulised adrenaline as a temporising measure.
  3. Call interventional pulmonology / ENT and anaesthesia now — definitive relief is urgent rigid bronchoscopy with debulking ± stenting.
  4. Do not sedate, paralyse, or lie the patient flat before the airway is controlled.

Recognise

Stridor (fixed intrathoracic obstruction may give expiratory stridor / monophonic wheeze), exertional then positional dyspnoea, cough, haemoptysis. Usually central lung, thyroid, oesophageal or mediastinal-nodal disease — intraluminal tumour, extrinsic compression, or both. Peri-arrest signs: exhaustion, accessory-muscle use, a quiet chest, falling SpO₂ with rising CO₂.

Confirm

In the decompensating patient, do not delay the interventional call for imaging. When stable: flexible nasendoscopy / bronchoscopy defines the level and whether it is intrinsic vs extrinsic; CT neck–thorax maps the lesion and distal airway patency; a flow–volume loop shows the obstruction pattern.

Manage

  • Temporise: sit up, heliox (lower gas density improves laminar flow past a fixed narrowing), high-flow humidified O₂, high-dose corticosteroid for oedema, nebulised adrenaline.
  • Definitive — rigid bronchoscopy is the workhorse: mechanical debulking, laser or cryo for intraluminal tumour; self-expanding or silicone stent for extrinsic compression. Flexible bronchoscopy for adjuncts only.
  • Consolidate: external-beam radiotherapy (or endobronchial brachytherapy) for radiosensitive tumour once the airway is secured; systemic therapy for chemosensitive disease.
  • Securing the airway: inhalational / awake technique with the interventionalist and rigid scope in the room; avoid routine paralysis or supine positioning, which can precipitate complete obstruction.

Disposition & escalation

Peri-arrest → theatre / bronchoscopy suite with thoracic-surgery or ENT + anaesthesia; HDU / ICU afterwards. Central airway obstruction usually marks advanced disease — agree a ceiling of care early, before the crash.

Pitfalls

  • Sedation, muscle relaxant, or lying the patient flat before the airway is controlled.
  • Labelling the stridor as "asthma / COPD" and reaching for bronchodilators alone.
  • Waiting on imaging in a decompensating patient instead of calling the interventionalist.

SourcesCHEST expert panel, Management of Central Airway Obstruction, Chest 2024 · Mudambi et al, J Thorac Dis 2017 (overview of malignant CAO). ESMO Handbook ch. 13 (Dyspnoea and Respiratory Failure).

Malignant bowel obstruction›
StructuralHigh

Mechanical or functional obstruction from intra-abdominal / peritoneal malignancy (commonly ovarian, colorectal) → colicky pain, vomiting, distension, absolute constipation.

Act now
  1. "Drip and suck": nil by mouth, wide-bore NG tube on free drainage, IV crystalloid; correct hypokalaemia / hyponatraemia.
  2. Analgesia (opioid) and antiemetic (haloperidol); stop prokinetics if there is colic or complete obstruction.
  3. Anti-secretory — hyoscine butylbromide 60–120 mg/24h CSCI, or octreotide 300–600 µg/24h SC/CSCI.
  4. Dexamethasone 6–16 mg/day IV (may hasten resolution); early joint surgical + oncology review.

Recognise

Colicky or constant abdominal pain, vomiting (faeculent when distal), distension, absolute constipation. Separate mechanical obstruction from functional causes (opioids, hypokalaemia), and single-level from multi-level disease — it drives whether surgery or a stent can help.

Confirm

Examine for localised tenderness / peritonism (strangulation or perforation). FBC, U&E — correct potassium and sodium. Erect chest / abdominal film, then CT abdomen–pelvis to fix the level and cause, single vs multi-level, and to triage surgery vs stent vs medical palliation.

Manage

  • Decompress & resuscitate: NBM, NG free drainage, IV crystalloid, correct electrolytes.
  • Symptom control (CSCI): analgesia (morphine / diamorphine); antiemetic — haloperidol first-line, or cyclizine / levomepromazine; anti-secretory — hyoscine butylbromide 60–120 mg/24h or octreotide 300–600 µg/24h (up to ~800); corticosteroid — dexamethasone 6–16 mg/day IV, which may bring about resolution.
  • Metoclopramide only in incomplete obstruction without colic — prokinesis worsens colic and is contraindicated in complete obstruction.
  • Definitive: surgery (bypass / resection / stoma) for fit patients with a single correctable mechanical level; self-expanding metallic stent for obstructing left-colonic or gastroduodenal lesions; medical palliation where surgery is inappropriate (diffuse peritoneal disease, poor performance status).

Disposition & escalation

Joint surgical / oncology / palliative-care decision — the choice is as much prognostic as technical. Venting gastrostomy for refractory vomiting when a long-term NG tube is unacceptable.

Pitfalls

  • Missing strangulation / perforation (fever, peritonism, rising lactate) behind a label of "inoperable obstruction".
  • Prokinetics in complete obstruction or colic.
  • Committing to "medical only" without CT — a single reversible level may be surgically or stent-correctable.

Obstructive uropathy & urological emergencies›
StructuralHigh

Malignant ureteric obstruction (pelvic / retroperitoneal tumour or nodes) → AKI, anuria, hyperkalaemia; or haemorrhagic cystitis with clot retention.

Act now
  1. Bilateral obstruction with AKI / anuria or urosepsis → urgent decompression: percutaneous nephrostomy or retrograde ureteric stent — do not wait.
  2. Check U&E and potassium; treat hyperkalaemia immediately.
  3. Haemorrhagic cystitis: 3-way catheter with continuous bladder irrigation, evacuate clots — mesna is prophylaxis, not treatment.
  4. Renal-tract ultrasound (hydronephrosis) ± CT; involve urology now; anticipate post-obstructive diuresis after relief.

Recognise

  • Obstructive uropathy: bilateral ureteric obstruction — or obstruction of a single functioning kidney — from pelvic / retroperitoneal tumour or nodal disease → AKI, anuria; hyperkalaemia and uraemia are what kill.
  • Haemorrhagic cystitis: visible haematuria / clot retention after cyclophosphamide or ifosfamide (acrolein toxicity), pelvic radiotherapy, or BK / adenovirus in the immunosuppressed.

Confirm

U&E, potassium, venous gas. Renal-tract ultrasound for hydronephrosis ± non-contrast CT for level and cause. Catheterise for retention or clot. If bleeding: FBC, coagulation, group & save.

Manage

  • Decompress obstruction: percutaneous nephrostomy or retrograde ureteric stent — nephrostomy is often preferred in malignant obstruction for higher technical success; stent where feasible. Treat hyperkalaemia and uraemic complications in parallel; dialysis if refractory.
  • Haemorrhagic cystitis: 3-way catheter with continuous bladder irrigation, manual / cystoscopic clot evacuation. Prophylaxis for ifosfamide (and high-dose cyclophosphamide) is hyperhydration plus mesna — total 60–100% of the oxazaphosphorine dose, e.g. 20% IV at 0, +4 and +8 h. Refractory bleeding → intravesical agents, cystoscopic diathermy, or arterial embolisation.
  • Post-obstructive diuresis: after relieving bilateral obstruction, monitor urine output and replace fluid and electrolytes — losses can be profound.

Disposition & escalation

Urology plus nephrology. In advanced disease, weigh whether decompression fits the goals of care — a nephrostomy in end-stage malignant obstruction warrants an honest conversation about what it buys.

Pitfalls

  • Discharging an anuric patient with bilateral obstruction — hyperkalaemia is rapidly fatal.
  • Giving mesna as treatment for established cystitis (it is prophylaxis only).
  • Under-replacing post-obstructive diuresis.

SourcesESMO Handbook ch. 9 (Renal Failure and Urological Emergencies) · ASCO chemotherapy uroprotectant guideline (Hensley et al, JCO 2009) for mesna dosing · urological-emergency reviews.

Tumour lysis syndrome›
MetabolicHigh

Massive tumour-cell turnover → hyperkalaemia, hyperphosphataemia, hyperuricaemia, hypocalcaemia ± AKI; peri-treatment in bulky / high-turnover disease.

Act now
  1. Aggressive isotonic IV fluids (~2500–3000 mL/m²/day; target urine output ≥2 mL/kg/h).
  2. Rasburicase 0.2 mg/kg IV for high-risk / established disease — hold if G6PD-deficient.
  3. Continuous cardiac monitoring; treat hyperkalaemia immediately.
  4. Bloods q4–6h (urate, K, PO₄, Ca, creatinine, LDH); involve nephrology early.

Recognise

Cairo–Bishop. Laboratory TLS = ≥2 of the following, from 3 days before to 7 days after therapy: uric acid ≥476 µmol/L (8 mg/dL) · K⁺ ≥6.0 mmol/L · phosphate ≥1.45 mmol/L (adults) · corrected Ca²⁺ ≤1.75 mmol/L. Clinical TLS = laboratory TLS + one of: AKI (creatinine ≥1.5×ULN), cardiac arrhythmia / sudden death, or seizure.

Howard (2011) refinements: require the two abnormalities simultaneously; the original 25%-change-from-baseline criterion is dropped (rarely meaningful unless already out of range); any symptomatic hypocalcaemia counts as clinical TLS.

Highest risk: Burkitt / lymphoblastic lymphoma, ALL and AML with high WBC, bulky chemosensitive tumours; compounded by pre-existing renal impairment, volume depletion, high LDH / urate.

Confirm

Urate, K, phosphate, corrected calcium, creatinine, LDH; ECG / telemetry; urine output.

Monitoring cadence: high-risk q4–6h · intermediate q8–12h · low-risk daily, through the at-risk period (regimen-dependent).

Manage

  • Risk-stratified prophylaxis: low → allopurinol 300 mg/day + hydration; intermediate → allopurinol + hydration (rasburicase if urate climbs); high / established → rasburicase 0.2 mg/kg IV daily + aggressive hydration. Do not combine allopurinol with rasburicase (allopurinol starves rasburicase of substrate).
  • Fluids: isotonic hyperhydration ~2500–3000 mL/m²/day (highest risk), target urine output ≥2 mL/kg/h; avoid routine urinary alkalinisation (worsens calcium-phosphate precipitation, especially when rasburicase is available); loop diuretic if urine output stays low despite optimal hydration.
  • Hyperkalaemia: calcium gluconate for cardioprotection, insulin–dextrose, salbutamol, binders; dialysis if refractory.
  • Hyperphosphataemia: phosphate binders.
  • Hypocalcaemia: treat only if symptomatic — giving calcium against a high phosphate risks metastatic calcification.

Grade

No true severity ladder — Cairo–Bishop distinguishes laboratory from clinical TLS (clinical = organ involvement); Howard 2011 offers a 0–IV clinical grade.

Disposition & escalation

High-risk patients → monitored bed / HDU. Renal replacement therapy for refractory hyperkalaemia, severe hyperphosphataemia / symptomatic hypocalcaemia, fluid overload, or oliguric AKI → nephrology / ICU (lower thresholds than for other AKI). Where feasible, control urate before cytoreduction in very-high-risk disease.

Pitfalls

  • Rasburicase in G6PD deficiency (haemolysis, methaemoglobinaemia) — screen high-risk ancestry first.
  • After rasburicase, uric-acid samples must go on ice or they degrade ex vivo (falsely low).
  • Over-alkalinisation. Treating asymptomatic hypocalcaemia.

Hypercalcaemia of malignancy›
MetabolicHigh

The commonest life-threatening metabolic emergency in cancer — usually PTHrP-driven (humoral) or osteolytic; dehydration, confusion, constipation, polyuria, AKI. Severity tracks the corrected calcium and the symptoms, not either alone.

Act now
  1. Confirm on corrected calcium (or ionised Ca); ECG (short QT, bradyarrhythmia).
  2. Rehydrate first: 0.9% saline IV ~200–500 mL/h to volume status — the immediate priority.
  3. Zoledronic acid 4 mg IV over ≥15 min once rehydrated (reduce/withhold in renal impairment; denosumab if bisphosphonate-refractory or CrCl <30).
  4. Calcitonin 4 units/kg SC/IM q12h for rapid short-term lowering while the bisphosphonate takes effect.

Recognise

Suspect in any cancer patient with new confusion, dehydration, constipation, polyuria/polydipsia or AKI. Symptoms track the rate of rise as much as the level.

Severity (corrected Ca): mild <3.0 mmol/L · moderate 3.0–3.4 · severe >3.4 mmol/L (or symptomatic at any level) — severe / symptomatic is the emergency.

Mechanism steers treatment: humoral (PTHrP, most solid tumours) · osteolytic (myeloma, breast) · 1,25-dihydroxy-vitamin-D-mediated (lymphoma, granulomatous disease — the one that responds to steroids).

Confirm

Corrected calcium = measured total Ca + 0.02 × (40 − albumin g/L); use ionised calcium where albumin or acid–base is deranged. PTH (suppressed in malignancy — distinguishes from primary hyperparathyroidism), PTHrP, phosphate, magnesium, renal function, ECG. Add 25-OH and 1,25-OH vitamin D if the cause is unclear (lymphoma / granuloma).

Manage

  • Rehydrate first: 0.9% saline ~200–500 mL/h titrated to volume status (commonly 3–4 L over 24 h); restores GFR and drives calciuresis. Watch for overload in cardiac / renal disease.
  • Zoledronic acid 4 mg IV over ≥15 min once volume-replete — nadir at day 4–7, effect lasts weeks. CrCl 30–60 mL/min: reduce to 3 mg; avoid if CrCl <30. Ensure hydration to limit nephrotoxicity.
  • Denosumab 120 mg SC (repeat day 8, day 15, then monthly) for bisphosphonate-refractory hypercalcaemia or where bisphosphonate is contraindicated by renal impairment — no renal dose adjustment, but watch for delayed and sometimes profound hypocalcaemia.
  • Calcitonin 4–8 units/kg SC/IM q6–12h for the first 24–48 h — acts within hours for a modest (~0.3–0.5 mmol/L) fall while the antiresorptive works; tachyphylaxis by 48–72 h.
  • Loop diuretics only for fluid overload after rehydration — not a calcium-lowering measure, and harmful in a volume-depleted patient.
  • Glucocorticoids (e.g. prednisolone 40–60 mg/day) for calcitriol-mediated hypercalcaemia of lymphoma / granulomatous disease.
  • Dialysis for severe hypercalcaemia with renal failure or refractory to the above.
  • Treat the malignancy — durable control needs effective anticancer therapy.

Disposition & escalation

Severe (>3.4 mmol/L) or symptomatic → monitored bed with telemetry and serial calcium / renal function. Nephrology for renal replacement in refractory disease or established renal failure. Recurrent hypercalcaemia signals uncontrolled disease — re-engage the treating oncology team.

Pitfalls

  • Giving a bisphosphonate before rehydrating — compounds nephrotoxicity.
  • Reaching for a loop diuretic as first-line — worsens the dehydration that drives the calcium up.
  • Reading an uncorrected calcium in a hypoalbuminaemic patient (falsely reassuring).
  • Missing delayed hypocalcaemia after denosumab — check calcium and correct vitamin D / magnesium.

Hyponatraemia / SIADH›
MetabolicHigh

The commonest electrolyte disturbance in cancer; SIADH (SCLC, CNS disease, chemotherapy) is the usual euvolaemic cause. Danger tracks the rate of fall and the symptoms — and the real hazard is correcting it too fast.

Act now
  1. Grade the symptoms (seizures / obtundation / vomiting = severe) and assess volume status.
  2. Severe symptomatic: 150 mL 3% hypertonic saline IV over 20 min, recheck Na, repeat ×1–2 until symptoms settle or Na has risen 5 mmol/L.
  3. Cap the rise: ≤10 mmol/L in 24 h and ≤18 mmol/L in 48 h (≤8 mmol/L/24 h if high demyelination risk) — over-correction causes osmotic demyelination.
  4. SIADH: fluid restriction and treat the cause; send paired serum + urine osmolality and urine sodium before you touch it.

Recognise

Symptom severity drives urgency: severe = seizures, coma / GCS drop, vomiting, cardiorespiratory distress · moderate = nausea, confusion, headache · mild = subtle / none. Symptoms reflect how fast the sodium fell, not just how low it is.

SIADH — Bartter–Schwartz criteria: clinical euvolaemia, serum osmolality <275 mOsm/kg, urine osmolality >100 mOsm/kg, urine sodium >30 mmol/L (on normal salt and water intake), no diuretic, and normal thyroid and adrenal function. Cancer causes: ectopic ADH (SCLC and others), CNS metastases, drugs (cyclophosphamide, vincristine, platinum), nausea, pain, opioids.

Confirm

Paired serum + urine osmolality and urine sodium; glucose (exclude translocational / pseudohyponatraemia); TFTs and 9 am cortisol (hypothyroidism and adrenal insufficiency mimic SIADH); careful volume assessment to separate hypovolaemic, euvolaemic and hypervolaemic causes.

Manage

  • Severe / symptomatic (any sodium level): 150 mL 3% saline over 20 min (≈2 mL/kg), recheck sodium, repeat until symptoms improve or sodium has risen 5 mmol/L. Target a 5 mmol/L rise in the first hour to relieve cerebral oedema, then stop the hypertonic saline and hold within the daily limit.
  • Correction limits: ≤10 mmol/L in the first 24 h and ≤18 mmol/L in the first 48 h. Aim lower — ≤8 mmol/L/24 h — where demyelination risk is high (sodium <105 mmol/L, hypokalaemia, alcohol-use disorder, malnutrition, advanced liver disease). If the limit is exceeded, re-lower with 5% dextrose ± desmopressin.
  • Chronic SIADH: fluid restriction <800–1000 mL/day is first line; if inadequate add oral urea, or a vasopressin-receptor antagonist (tolvaptan) with caution — vaptans risk over-rapid correction and are hepatotoxic (avoid in liver disease and beyond 30 days).
  • Treat the cause: systemic therapy for SCLC, withdraw the offending drug.

Disposition & escalation

Severe symptomatic hyponatraemia → HDU / monitored bed with sodium checked every 2–4 h during active correction. Involve endocrinology / nephrology for refractory SIADH or diagnostic uncertainty, and if over-correction needs active re-lowering.

Pitfalls

  • Correcting chronic hyponatraemia too quickly — the demyelination risk, not the low number, is what harms the patient.
  • Starting a vaptan while fluid-restricting, or in liver disease.
  • Labelling it SIADH without excluding hypothyroidism and adrenal insufficiency.
  • Treating the sodium value rather than the patient — an asymptomatic chronic 125 mmol/L does not need hypertonic saline.

Related: cerebral / leptomeningeal disease may co-drive the picture — see raised-ICP crises (this module).

Acute transfusion reactions›
Infective / haematologicalHigh

Any new fever, hypotension, dyspnoea, rash or pain during or shortly after a transfusion. The dangerous reactions — ABO haemolysis, bacterial sepsis, anaphylaxis, TRALI — present early and can look identical to a benign febrile reaction at onset.

Act now
  1. STOP the transfusion; keep the line open with 0.9% saline.
  2. Recheck patient identity against the unit and compatibility label — ABO incompatibility is a bedside clerical failure.
  3. ABC and obs; escalate by picture — IM adrenaline 0.5 mg for anaphylaxis, fluids ± vasopressors for hypotension, O₂ for hypoxia.
  4. Return the unit + giving set to the lab; send FBC, repeat group & crossmatch, DAT, LDH, haptoglobin, bilirubin, coagulation, and blood cultures (patient and unit).

Recognise — the acute reactions and their tells

  • Acute haemolytic (ABO incompatibility): fever, loin / infusion-site pain, hypotension, dark urine, DIC, oliguria — minutes into the transfusion. A medical emergency.
  • Febrile non-haemolytic (FNHTR): isolated fever ± rigors, haemodynamically stable — a diagnosis of exclusion.
  • Allergic → anaphylactic: urticaria / pruritus (mild) escalating to bronchospasm, angio-oedema and hypotension (severe; consider IgA deficiency).
  • TACO vs TRALI (both dyspnoea + hypoxia within ~6 h): TACO — circulatory overload: hypertension, raised JVP, positive fluid balance, responds to diuretics; risk in elderly / cardiac / renal patients. TRALI — non-cardiogenic: hypotension, fever, normal JVP, bilateral infiltrates, no response to diuretics; respiratory support and notify the blood service.
  • Bacterial contamination / septic: rapid high fever, rigors, hypotension, collapse — most associated with platelets (stored at room temperature).

Confirm

Clerical bedside check first. Haemolysis: DAT, repeat crossmatch, LDH, haptoglobin, bilirubin, free haemoglobin, urinalysis. Sepsis: paired blood cultures (patient and unit) and Gram stain of the unit. Respiratory: CXR to separate TACO from TRALI; BNP and fluid balance support TACO.

Manage (per type)

  • Mild febrile: paracetamol; slow the transfusion and observe closely, or restart cautiously if the fever settles and no other features.
  • Mild allergic (urticaria only): antihistamine; may restart slowly under observation.
  • Anaphylaxis: stop permanently, IM adrenaline 0.5 mg (500 µg) repeated every 5 min as needed, O₂, IV fluids, adjunctive antihistamine / steroid; use washed or IgA-deficient components in future.
  • Acute haemolytic: stop, aggressive IV fluids to maintain urine output, support for DIC and AKI; treat as an ABO error until disproven and inform the lab and haematology.
  • TACO: stop / slow, sit the patient up, O₂, IV furosemide; if transfusion is still needed, restart at a reduced rate with diuretic cover.
  • TRALI: stop, respiratory support (may need ventilation), supportive care — do not diurese; notify the blood service (implicated donor).
  • Septic: stop, cultures (patient + unit), broad-spectrum IV antibiotics, resuscitate; return the unit for Gram stain and culture.

Disposition & escalation

Haemodynamic or respiratory compromise → ICU. Notify the hospital transfusion laboratory and team early, and report serious reactions to the national haemovigilance scheme (SHOT / SABRE equivalent). Do not issue further components for that patient until the cause is understood.

Pitfalls

  • Not stopping for "just a fever" — early ABO haemolysis and bacterial sepsis are indistinguishable from a benign febrile reaction at onset.
  • Diuresing TRALI (it is not volume overload).
  • Skipping the bedside clerical check — the root cause of most fatal ABO errors.
  • Discarding the unit before it can be cultured / Gram-stained.

Related: DIC (this module) may complicate acute haemolytic reactions; febrile neutropenia (this module) overlaps the septic differential.

Disseminated intravascular coagulation›
Infective / haematologicalHigh

Consumptive coagulopathy driven by an underlying trigger — sepsis, APL / other malignancy, obstetric catastrophe, trauma; simultaneous microvascular thrombosis and bleeding. There is no DIC to treat without a precipitant to treat.

Act now
  1. Identify and treat the precipitant now — sepsis (source control + antibiotics), APL, solid tumour. The only definitive therapy.
  2. Send a DIC screen: platelets, PT/APTT, fibrinogen, D-dimer/FDP — and repeat serially (DIC is dynamic).
  3. Score with the ISTH overt-DIC criteria (≥5 = overt DIC).
  4. Support with blood products guided by bleeding + trajectory, not by lab numbers alone.

Recognise

Bleeding (oozing from cannulae / wounds, mucosal, ecchymoses) and/or thrombosis with organ dysfunction. Suspect in septic shock, acute promyelocytic leukaemia and other acute leukaemias, and metastatic mucin-secreting adenocarcinoma. Chronic compensated DIC (solid tumours) often presents with thrombosis — Trousseau syndrome — rather than bleeding.

Confirm — ISTH overt-DIC score

Apply only when there is an underlying disorder known to be associated with DIC.

  • Platelets >100 = 0 · 50–100 = 1 · <50 = 2
  • Fibrin marker (D-dimer / FDP): no rise = 0 · moderate = 2 · strong = 3
  • PT prolongation <3 s = 0 · 3–6 s = 1 · >6 s = 2
  • Fibrinogen >1 g/L = 0 · <1 g/L = 1

≥5 = overt DIC (repeat the score daily); <5 = suggestive of non-overt DIC — repeat in 1–2 days. The score supports the diagnosis; it does not replace the clinical picture or the serial trend.

Manage

  • Treat the trigger — antibiotics and source control for sepsis; start ATRA immediately for suspected APL. Nothing else reverses DIC.
  • Blood products only if bleeding, high bleeding risk, or pre-procedure:
    • Platelets if bleeding and <50 × 10⁹/L, or not bleeding and <20 × 10⁹/L.
    • FFP if bleeding with PT / APTT >1.5 × normal.
    • Fibrinogen concentrate or cryoprecipitate if fibrinogen stays <1.5 g/L despite FFP.
  • Heparin: prophylactic-dose LMWH for VTE prophylaxis in non-bleeding DIC; therapeutic anticoagulation only where thrombosis dominates (Trousseau, purpura fulminans) — not routine, and not while bleeding.
  • Do not chase lab numbers in a stable, non-bleeding patient.

Disposition & escalation

Haemodynamic instability or major bleeding → ICU. Urgent haematology involvement, and for suspected APL an immediate haematology referral — this is a haematological emergency, not a next-day problem.

Pitfalls

  • APL-DIC: start ATRA on clinical suspicion, before genetic confirmation, and support the coagulopathy aggressively (platelets >30–50 × 10⁹/L, fibrinogen >1.5 g/L). The early coagulopathy — not leukostasis — is the killer; avoid unnecessary invasive procedures.
  • Transfusing to a lab target in a non-bleeding patient.
  • Missing APL behind the coagulopathy in a new acute leukaemia.
  • Withholding VTE prophylaxis in non-bleeding DIC.
  • Expecting the numbers to correct before the precipitant is controlled — they will not.

Related: febrile neutropenia / septic shock (this module) is the commonest DIC trigger; acute haemolytic transfusion reactions (this module) can precipitate DIC.

Febrile neutropaenia &amp; septic shock›
Infective / haematologicalHigh

Fever in a neutropenic patient — a medical emergency; treat as sepsis until proven otherwise, and step up to septic-shock management the moment hypotension or hypoperfusion appears (then peri-arrest).

Act now
  1. Empiric antipseudomonal β-lactam within 1 hour — piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV, cefepime 2 g IV, or meropenem 1–2 g IV. Do not wait for the count or cultures.
  2. Draw blood cultures (peripheral + every central lumen), lactate, FBC and renal/liver panel first if it costs no time — but the antibiotic does not wait for them.
  3. If hypotensive or lactate ≥2 mmol/L: Surviving Sepsis bundle — ≥30 mL/kg crystalloid over 3 h, norepinephrine to MAP ≥65 mmHg, source control.
  4. Add vancomycin only for a specific indication (see Manage) — not routinely.

Recognise

Neutropenia: ANC <0.5 ×10⁹/L, or <1.0 ×10⁹/L and expected to fall below 0.5 within 48 h.

Fever: a single oral temperature ≥38.3 °C, or ≥38.0 °C sustained over 1 h.

One fever is the trigger — there is often no localising sign and no source. Highest risk with ANC <0.1 ×10⁹/L, anticipated neutropenia >7 days, and haematological malignancy or HSCT. Watch for the shift to septic shock: hypotension, lactate ≥2 mmol/L, mottling, new confusion.

Confirm

Blood cultures from a peripheral vein and from each central lumen; FBC, renal and liver panel, lactate; urinalysis, CXR and site swabs directed by symptoms. Do not delay the antibiotic for the workup.

Risk-stratify (MASCC index). Score ≥21 = low risk of complications and a candidate for the oral / outpatient pathway; <21 = high risk → admit for IV therapy. The index sums burden of illness, no hypotension, no COPD, solid tumour or no prior fungal infection, no dehydration, outpatient onset, and age <60.

Manage

  • First dose within 1 h — antipseudomonal β-lactam monotherapy: piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV q6–8h, cefepime 2 g IV q8h, or meropenem 1–2 g IV q8h, dosed to renal function. Carbapenem first-line if a resistant organism is likely.
  • Add vancomycin only for a named indication: haemodynamic instability / severe sepsis, suspected catheter-related infection, skin/soft-tissue infection, pneumonia, known MRSA colonisation, or Gram-positive cocci in blood pending identification. Stop at 48–72 h if not confirmed.
  • Low risk (MASCC ≥21), stable, tolerating oral: oral ciprofloxacin + amoxicillin-clavulanate with a defined pathway for review and readmission — not if the patient was on fluoroquinolone prophylaxis.
  • Septic shock: Surviving Sepsis bundle — ≥30 mL/kg balanced crystalloid over 3 h, norepinephrine first-line to MAP ≥65 mmHg, repeat lactate, remove an infected line. Broaden for resistant organisms and add antifungal cover if not settling.
  • G-CSF is not routine treatment of established FN — its risk-directed use is prophylactic (see Tox).

Disposition & escalation

High risk (MASCC <21) → admit for IV antibiotics; profound or prolonged neutropenia → monitored bed. Shock, rising lactate, or organ dysfunction → ICU. Reassess at 48–72 h: de-escalate on culture results, continue until afebrile with a recovering ANC. Involve infection and haematology early.

Pitfalls

  • Delaying the first dose for cultures, imaging, or line access — start within the hour.
  • Reflex vancomycin in every patient; reserve it for a named indication and stop if unconfirmed.
  • Missing evolving shock behind a 'stable' febrile patient — recheck lactate and perfusion.
  • Applying MASCC to an already-unstable patient; the index assumes an uncomplicated presentation.

Related: G-CSF and febrile-neutropenia-risk prophylaxis are owned by Tox — Acute manages the acute episode; see Tox for primary/secondary G-CSF prophylaxis and FN-risk chemotherapy stratification.

Neutropenic enterocolitis (typhlitis)›
Infective / haematologicalHigh

Fever, right-lower-quadrant / abdominal pain and diarrhoea in a neutropenic patient — inflammation and necrosis of the caecum and bowel wall; high mortality if missed.

Act now
  1. Broad-spectrum antibiotics with anaerobic and antipseudomonal Gram-negative cover — piperacillin-tazobactam or a carbapenem (or cefepime plus metronidazole).
  2. Bowel rest, nasogastric decompression, IV fluids and electrolyte correction.
  3. Urgent CT abdomen/pelvis; serial abdominal examinations; surgical review early.
  4. No antimotility / antidiarrhoeal agents.

Recognise

The triad of fever, abdominal pain (classically right lower quadrant, over the caecum) and diarrhoea during neutropenia — typically 1–2 weeks after cytotoxic chemotherapy (high-dose cytarabine, taxanes, intensive induction). Distension, rebound, peritonism or GI bleeding signal transmural necrosis and impending perforation.

Confirm

CT abdomen/pelvis is the test of choice — circumferential bowel-wall thickening (caecum ± ascending colon), mural oedema, pericolic fat stranding, ± pneumatosis. FBC, lactate, coagulation and blood cultures; send stool for Clostridioides difficile (the key mimic). Avoid colonoscopy — perforation risk in inflamed, neutropenic bowel.

Manage

  • Antibiotics with anaerobic cover: piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV q6–8h or meropenem 1 g IV q8h; if using cefepime, add metronidazole for anaerobes. Add antifungal cover if not improving or persistently neutropenic.
  • Supportive: bowel rest, nasogastric tube for ileus/obstruction, IV fluids, correction of electrolytes and coagulopathy, transfusion to target.
  • Neutrophil recovery drives outcome — G-CSF is commonly used to shorten the neutropenic window.
  • Avoid antimotility agents (loperamide, opioids) — they promote stasis, toxin retention and perforation.

Disposition & escalation

Admit to a monitored bed with serial examinations and imaging. Involve surgery early but operate selectively — surgery is reserved for perforation, uncontrolled GI bleeding, or clinical deterioration / suspected necrosis despite maximal medical therapy. Most cases resolve with neutrophil recovery and conservative care; ICU for haemodynamic instability.

Pitfalls

  • Missing it — abdominal signs are blunted in neutropenia; keep a low threshold for CT.
  • Omitting anaerobic cover.
  • Antimotility agents, or early colonoscopy.
  • Not excluding C. difficile.

Hyperviscosity syndrome / leukostasis›
Infective / haematologicalHigh

Two flow emergencies — paraprotein hyperviscosity (Waldenström / myeloma) and leukostasis (acute leukaemia with a very high blast count). Both cause visual, neurological and mucosal / respiratory compromise; both need urgent cytoreduction of the offending load.

Act now
  1. Paraprotein hyperviscosity: urgent plasmapheresis (plasma exchange) and treat the underlying clone — do not delay for viscosity confirmation if symptomatic.
  2. Leukostasis: urgent cytoreduction — hydroxyurea and/or leukapheresis, and move to induction chemotherapy; start TLS prophylaxis (hydration + rasburicase/allopurinol).
  3. Avoid RBC transfusion until cytoreduced — added red cells raise viscosity and can worsen occlusion. Correct thrombocytopenia and coagulopathy.
  4. Fundoscopy, neuro and respiratory assessment; senior haematology now.

Recognise

Paraprotein hyperviscosity — high circulating monoclonal protein, usually IgM in Waldenström macroglobulinaemia (also IgA / IgG myeloma). The classic triad: mucosal bleeding (epistaxis, gum bleeding), visual disturbance (retinopathy with dilated 'sausage-link' veins, haemorrhages), and neurological signs (headache, vertigo, confusion, coma). Symptoms are usual once serum viscosity exceeds ~4 cP, though the threshold varies between patients.

Leukostasis — symptomatic microvascular plugging by leukaemic blasts, typically AML with WBC >100 ×10⁹/L (some series flag risk from ~50–75; ALL and chronic leukaemias tolerate far higher counts). Presents with hypoxia / dyspnoea and pulmonary infiltrates, and neurological signs (confusion, visual change, headache); fever is common. Hyperleukocytosis is the lab finding; leukostasis is the clinical syndrome.

Confirm

Hyperviscosity: serum viscosity, immunoglobulins / paraprotein quantitation, serum protein electrophoresis; fundoscopy; FBC, renal panel, calcium. Treat on clinical grounds — do not await viscosity if the patient is symptomatic.

Leukostasis: FBC and blast count, film, coagulation and fibrinogen (exclude DIC, especially APL), LDH / urate / potassium / phosphate / calcium and creatinine (TLS), ABG and CXR. It is a clinical diagnosis — imaging supports, it does not exclude.

Manage

  • Paraprotein hyperviscosity: urgent therapeutic plasma exchange removes the paraprotein (largely intravascular, so a modest fall in concentration drops viscosity sharply) and buys time; follow immediately with definitive treatment of the clone. Repeat exchange as symptoms dictate.
  • Leukostasis: cytoreduce urgently — hydroxyurea can start before the leukaemia subtype is confirmed; leukapheresis lowers the count rapidly in symptomatic hyperleukocytosis (adjunct, with no proven survival benefit) and is avoided in acute promyelocytic leukaemia (worsens coagulopathy). Definitive control is prompt induction chemotherapy.
  • TLS prophylaxis for leukostasis — IV hydration plus rasburicase (high risk) or allopurinol, with close electrolyte monitoring.
  • Transfusion: withhold red cells until the count / paraprotein is reduced unless there is critical anaemia; platelets and FFP as needed for bleeding or coagulopathy.

Disposition & escalation

Both are haematological emergencies for a monitored bed and immediate haematology involvement; arrange apheresis (plasma exchange or leukapheresis) urgently. ICU for respiratory failure or reduced consciousness. Leukostasis carries high early mortality — cytoreduction and induction must not wait.

Pitfalls

  • Transfusing red cells into an untreated hyperviscous / hyperleukocytic patient.
  • Waiting for a viscosity result before exchanging a symptomatic patient.
  • Forgetting TLS prophylaxis when cytoreducing a high blast load.
  • Leukapheresis in APL — it aggravates the coagulopathy; prioritise ATRA and support.

Related: TLS (this module, Metabolic) — leukostasis and its treatment precipitate tumour lysis; run TLS prophylaxis in parallel.

Major haemorrhage (incl. massive haemoptysis)›
Infective / haematologicalPeri-arrest

Uncontrolled or life-threatening bleeding — resuscitate, activate the major-haemorrhage protocol, reverse anticoagulation and get to definitive source control. Massive haemoptysis kills by asphyxiation before exsanguination.

Act now
  1. ABC: protect the airway, high-flow oxygen, two large-bore cannulae, cross-match; activate the major-haemorrhage protocol.
  2. Transfuse in a fixed ratio (~1:1:1 red cells : FFP : platelets) until laboratory-guided; give calcium; keep warm; avoid over-crystalloid.
  3. Reverse anticoagulation by agent (see Manage); tranexamic acid 1 g IV over 10 min then 1 g over 8 h in trauma / surgical / obstetric bleeding.
  4. Definitive source control — surgery, interventional radiology, or endoscopy — without delay.

Recognise

Haemorrhage that threatens life by volume or by site: haemodynamic instability, ongoing visible loss, or bleeding into a critical space (airway, cranium, pericardium). In cancer patients suspect tumour erosion of a vessel, thrombocytopenia, DIC, or anticoagulant effect. Massive haemoptysis (large-volume or any volume compromising gas exchange) is an airway emergency first — a small amount of blood can flood and asphyxiate.

Confirm

FBC, coagulation screen with fibrinogen, group-and-save / cross-match, U&E, calcium, lactate and a gas; a drug history for anticoagulants / antiplatelets and the last dose. Localise the source — imaging or endoscopy as the patient allows; for haemoptysis, CT angiography and bronchoscopy identify the bleeding side. Resuscitation and the workup run in parallel.

Manage

  • Resuscitate: major-haemorrhage protocol with fixed-ratio product delivery (~1:1:1), moving to laboratory-guided replacement (fibrinogen, platelets, coagulation) as results return; ionised calcium replacement; active warming; permissive hypotension until source control where appropriate.
  • Reverse anticoagulation by agent: warfarin → IV vitamin K + four-factor prothrombin complex concentrate; dabigatran → idarucizumab; factor Xa inhibitors (apixaban / rivaroxaban / edoxaban) → andexanet alfa, or 4-factor PCC where it is unavailable; heparin → protamine. Give platelets for a bleeding thrombocytopenic or antiplatelet-treated patient.
  • Tranexamic acid: 1 g IV over 10 min then 1 g over 8 h — benefit established in trauma (give within 3 h), and in surgical and obstetric haemorrhage. Not for GI bleeding (no mortality benefit, increased VTE).
  • Massive haemoptysis: protect the airway (early intubation if flooding); position bleeding side down to shield the good lung; consider selective intubation of the non-bleeding lung; urgent bronchoscopy to localise / tamponade; bronchial artery embolisation is first-line for control, surgery if refractory.
  • Source control is the priority throughout — surgery, IR embolisation, or endoscopic therapy; products buy time, they do not fix the source.

Disposition & escalation

Peri-arrest — call the resuscitation / major-haemorrhage team, transfusion laboratory, and the relevant proceduralist (surgery, IR, endoscopy, thoracics) simultaneously. ICU / theatre for ongoing instability. Monitor the coagulation and calcium through resuscitation; correct hypothermia and acidosis, which perpetuate bleeding.

Pitfalls

  • Crystalloid resuscitation instead of blood products — dilutes clotting factors and worsens coagulopathy.
  • Tranexamic acid reflexively in GI bleeding (HALT-IT: no benefit, more thrombosis).
  • Forgetting the anticoagulant — always take the drug and last-dose history and reverse specifically.
  • Treating massive haemoptysis as a lung problem before it is an airway problem; secure the airway and position first.
  • Letting hypothermia, acidosis and hypocalcaemia set in during large-volume transfusion.

Acute cancer-associated VTE / pulmonary embolism›
VascularHigh

Cancer-associated DVT or PE (Trousseau) — high recurrence and high bleeding risk pull in opposite directions; agent choice turns on tumour site and bleeding risk.

Act now
  1. Anticoagulate: weight-based LMWH, or a DOAC — apixaban, rivaroxaban, or edoxaban (edoxaban only after ≥5 days of LMWH).
  2. Haemodynamically unstable (high-risk) PE → systemic thrombolysis unless contraindicated — the bleeding risk is higher in cancer.
  3. Before choosing an agent, weigh a GI / GU primary and bleeding risk; check platelets and renal function.
  4. For thrombocytopenia, dose-reduce or hold rather than stop.

Recognise

New dyspnoea, pleuritic pain, unilateral leg swelling, unexplained tachycardia, hypoxia or syncope in a cancer patient. Incidental PE found on staging CT still warrants treatment. High-risk (massive) PE = obstructive shock / hypotension.

Confirm

CTPA for PE, compression ultrasound for DVT. ECG, troponin and echo (RV strain) identify high-risk PE. FBC, renal function and baseline coagulation guide agent and dose.

Manage

Anticoagulate for at least 6 months while the cancer is active. Options:

  • LMWH (e.g. dalteparin 200 IU/kg OD for 1 month, then 150 IU/kg OD) — the long-standing standard, and preferred in luminal GI / GU cancer or high bleeding risk.
  • Apixaban 10 mg BID ×7 days, then 5 mg BID — CARAVAGGIO: recurrent VTE 5.6% vs 7.9% with dalteparin (HR 0.63; 95% CI 0.37–1.07), major bleeding 3.8% vs 4.0% (HR 0.82; 0.40–1.69), with no excess major bleeding.
  • Edoxaban 60 mg OD after ≥5 days of LMWH — Hokusai-VTE Cancer: composite recurrent VTE / major bleeding 12.8% vs 13.5% (HR 0.97; 0.70–1.36); less recurrent VTE (7.9% vs 11.3%) but more major bleeding (6.9% vs 4.0%; risk difference +2.9 points, 0.1–5.6), the excess largely upper-GI in patients with GI cancer.
  • Rivaroxaban 15 mg BID ×3 weeks, then 20 mg OD — SELECT-D: 6-month VTE recurrence 4% vs 11% (HR 0.43; 0.19–0.99), but more clinically-relevant non-major bleeding (13% vs 4%; HR 3.76; 1.63–8.69).

High-risk (massive) PE: systemic thrombolysis for haemodynamic instability; catheter-directed therapy or embolectomy where thrombolysis is contraindicated — cancer raises the bleeding risk on both sides of the decision.

Thrombocytopenia: reduce or hold the dose rather than stop; guideline platelet thresholds sit around 25–50 ×10⁹/L — follow local protocol.

Disposition & escalation

High-risk PE → HDU / ICU on a thrombolysis pathway. Recurrent VTE on anticoagulation → escalate the LMWH dose; reserve an IVC filter for a genuine, ongoing contraindication to anticoagulation, and retrieve it when possible.

Pitfalls

  • DOACs in luminal GI or GU cancers — higher GI / GU bleeding (Hokusai, SELECT-D); prefer apixaban or LMWH there.
  • Treating incidental PE as benign — recurrence risk is comparable, so it is anticoagulated.
  • Defaulting to an IVC filter instead of anticoagulation.
  • Stopping anticoagulation for mild thrombocytopenia rather than dose-adjusting.

Cytokine release syndrome›
Treatment-relatedHighASTCT 1–4post-2016

Supraphysiological cytokine surge after CAR-T or T-cell–engaging bispecifics; fever ± hypotension / hypoxia, usually within days of infusion.

Act now
  1. Recognise: fever ≥38 °C after immune-effector-cell therapy — exclude / treat neutropenic sepsis in parallel.
  2. Grade by ASTCT (see below).
  3. Tocilizumab 8 mg/kg IV (max 800 mg) for grade ≥2 (and persistent grade 1).
  4. Supportive: fluids for hypotension, O₂; add dexamethasone for tocilizumab-refractory or grade ≥3; ICU for grade 3–4.

Recognise

Onset typically 1–14 days after CAR-T (often day 2–3), can be within hours with bispecifics. Fever is the defining feature, then hypotension and/or hypoxia. Concurrent infection mimics and coexists — screen and cover. May co-occur with ICANS.

Confirm

Obs (temp, BP, SpO₂), sepsis screen (cultures, lactate), FBC, renal / liver panel, CRP and ferritin (trend; non-specific), coagulation (CRS can drive coagulopathy / HLH). Continuous monitoring.

Grade — ASTCT 2019

  • G1 fever ≥38 °C (no hypotension, no hypoxia).
  • G2 fever + hypotension not requiring vasopressors, and/or hypoxia needing low-flow O₂ (≤6 L/min).
  • G3 fever + hypotension needing one vasopressor (± vasopressin), and/or hypoxia needing high-flow O₂ (>6 L/min).
  • G4 fever + hypotension needing multiple vasopressors (excl. vasopressin), and/or hypoxia needing positive-pressure ventilation.

After antipyretics / tocilizumab, fever may resolve — then grade on hypotension / hypoxia.

Manage (grade-directed)

  • G1: supportive, antipyretics, fluids; empiric antibiotics if neutropenic; tocilizumab if persistent / prolonged.
  • G2: tocilizumab 8 mg/kg IV (max 800 mg), repeatable q8h (≤3 doses/24h, ≤4 total); fluid boluses; O₂; add dexamethasone 10 mg IV if inadequate response.
  • G3: tocilizumab + dexamethasone 10 mg IV q6h; vasopressor; ICU.
  • G4: tocilizumab + high-dose methylprednisolone (1 g/day); ICU, multiple pressors / ventilation; anakinra or alternative anti-cytokine for refractory disease.

Disposition & escalation

G1–2 → monitored ward / HDU per protocol; G3–4 → ICU. Notify the cellular-therapy team and follow the product / institutional protocol. Watch for concurrent ICANS and HLH / MAS.

Pitfalls

  • Missing neutropenic sepsis behind the fever (treat both).
  • Withholding tocilizumab in hypotensive CRS.
  • Steroids at recommended dosing do not appear to compromise CAR-T efficacy — do not withhold when indicated.
  • Tocilizumab does not treat ICANS (poor CNS penetration — use steroids there).

Anaphylaxis, hypersensitivity & infusion reactions›
Treatment-relatedPeri-arrest

Acute reaction during or shortly after a systemic agent — spanning a mild infusion reaction to airway/circulatory collapse; treat as anaphylaxis the moment airway, breathing or circulation is threatened.

Act now
  1. STOP the infusion; keep IV access, call for help.
  2. Anaphylaxis → adrenaline 500 µg IM (0.5 mL 1:1000) into the anterolateral thigh; repeat every 5 min if no improvement.
  3. Airway + high-flow O₂; lay flat with legs raised (sit up if breathing is the problem); IV crystalloid bolus for hypotension.
  4. Adjuncts after adrenaline, not instead of it: antihistamine and corticosteroid.

Recognise

Anaphylaxis = acute onset with a life-threatening Airway (swelling, stridor, hoarseness), Breathing (wheeze, hypoxia) or Circulation (hypotension, collapse) problem, usually with skin changes (urticaria, angio-oedema) — but skin signs are absent in up to a fifth, so their absence does not exclude it.

Distinguish the mechanism — it changes what you do next:

  • Standard infusion-related reaction (mAbs — rituximab, cetuximab; often first infusion): fever, chills, flushing, rigors, back pain, mild dyspnoea from cytokine release. Usually settles with stopping/slowing and premedication.
  • True IgE-mediated hypersensitivity: taxanes (frequently the first exposure, a reaction to the Cremophor/polysorbate vehicle) and platinums — a genuine IgE hypersensitivity that characteristically emerges after several cycles (typically the 6th+ carboplatin exposure) once the patient is sensitised.

Cetuximab reactions in the first exposure can be IgE-mediated (anti-α-gal antibodies) and severe.

Confirm

Clinical diagnosis — do not delay adrenaline for tests. Continuous obs (BP, SpO₂, ECG). Serial mast-cell tryptase (as soon as safe, ~1–2 h, and a baseline at ≥24 h) supports a later diagnosis of anaphylaxis but never guides the acute response. Document the culprit drug, cycle number and timing for the allergy/immunology referral.

Manage

  • Adrenaline is the only first-line drug: 500 µg IM (0.5 mL of 1:1000) anterolateral thigh, repeated every 5 min to response. IV adrenaline (titrated, e.g. 50 µg boluses) is for refractory shock in a monitored setting by those trained to use it — the IM route first, every time.
  • Airway / breathing: high-flow O₂, nebulised salbutamol for bronchospasm, nebulised adrenaline for upper-airway oedema; call anaesthetics early for a threatened airway.
  • Circulation: lie flat, legs up; rapid IV crystalloid bolus (500–1000 mL, repeat as needed); refractory hypotension → escalate to critical care for an adrenaline infusion ± further vasopressors.
  • Adjuncts (second-line, after adrenaline): a non-sedating antihistamine and a corticosteroid — they treat the cutaneous component and may blunt a biphasic reaction, but neither is life-saving and neither replaces adrenaline.
  • Standard infusion reaction (not anaphylaxis): stop, treat symptoms; once settled, restart at a slower rate with premedication.

Grade

No single oncology scale governs the acute call — sensitivity to the airway/breathing/circulation threat is what triggers the anaphylaxis pathway. Retrospectively, infusion reactions are graded by CTCAE and hypersensitivity severity by the Ring & Messmer / Brown grades; those inform rechallenge planning, not the resuscitation.

Disposition & escalation

Post-anaphylaxis: observe for a biphasic reaction — 6–12 h for a severe or refractory reaction, shorter (≥2 h) for a rapidly-resolving one; monitored bed / HDU if adrenaline was repeated or shock occurred. Refer to allergy/immunology before the next cycle.

Future cycles depend on the mechanism: a mild infusion reaction → premedicate and slow the rate. A confirmed IgE hypersensitivity to a drug that remains the best treatment (platinum, taxane) → formal graded desensitisation in a monitored setting, not simple rechallenge — desensitisation induces temporary tolerance and must be repeated each cycle.

Pitfalls

  • Reaching for antihistamine/steroid first and delaying adrenaline — the commonest fatal error in anaphylaxis.
  • Giving adrenaline IV or subcutaneously as the default — IM into the thigh is the standard route.
  • Sitting a hypotensive patient up (or standing them up) — can precipitate fatal circulatory collapse; keep them flat.
  • Treating a genuine platinum hypersensitivity as a rate problem — the reaction worsens on rechallenge; it needs desensitisation.

Vesicant chemotherapy extravasation›
Treatment-relatedUrgentGrade by vesicant

Vesicant leaks from the vein into surrounding tissue — pain, swelling, burning or loss of blood return at the cannula; anthracyclines and vinca alkaloids can cause progressive necrosis if mismanaged.

Act now
  1. STOP the infusion immediately; do not remove the cannula yet.
  2. Disconnect and aspirate residual drug through the cannula, then remove it.
  3. Elevate the limb; mark and photograph the area; do not flush or apply pressure.
  4. Identify the agent → anthracycline = COLD pack + dexrazoxane; vinca alkaloid = WARM pack + hyaluronidase.

Recognise

Suspect extravasation with any burning/stinging pain, swelling, erythema, induration or loss of blood return during a vesicant infusion — symptoms may be subtle and can be masked by a central line. Vesicants (tissue necrosis): anthracyclines (doxorubicin, epirubicin, daunorubicin), vinca alkaloids (vincristine, vinblastine, vinorelbine), and others (mitomycin C, dactinomycin, trabectedin). Irritants (inflammation without necrosis): platinums, taxanes, etoposide — lower stakes but still stop and assess.

Confirm

Clinical. Record the drug, estimated volume, cannula site and time; outline the affected area and photograph it for serial comparison. Early plastics/surgical review for a large-volume anthracycline extravasation or any blistering/necrosis.

Manage

General (every vesicant): stop, disconnect, aspirate residual drug through the still-sited cannula, then remove; elevate; do not flush the line or apply direct pressure.

  • Anthracyclines → COLD + dexrazoxane. Intermittent cold packs (~20 min, several times daily) to vasoconstrict and localise the drug — not warm. Dexrazoxane IV for significant extravasation: 1000 mg/m² day 1, 1000 mg/m² day 2, 500 mg/m² day 3 (cap at BSA 2 m²; reduce 50% if CrCl <40 mL/min), first dose within 6 h and each day at the same hour, into a different limb. Remove the cold pack 15 min before and during the dexrazoxane infusion (vasoconstriction impairs delivery). Topical DMSO is an alternative where dexrazoxane is unavailable — do not combine with dexrazoxane.
  • Vinca alkaloids → WARM + hyaluronidase. Intermittent warm compresses to disperse and dilute the drug (the opposite of anthracyclines — the classic trap), plus hyaluronidase 150–1500 U SC around the site to break down tissue matrix and aid dispersion. Cold is contraindicated here.
  • Mitomycin C / mitoxantrone → topical DMSO to the area, air-dried, repeated over several days.

Grade

No single severity ladder — management is driven by the drug class (vesicant vs irritant, and which vesicant), which sets the compress temperature and the antidote. Severity is then judged clinically by tissue changes over the following days (erythema → blistering → ulceration/necrosis).

Disposition & escalation

Surgical / plastics referral for blistering, progressive pain, ulceration, or any large anthracycline extravasation despite dexrazoxane — debridement/reconstruction may be needed. Document fully, notify the treating team, and complete an incident report; review with pharmacy before the next cycle (line choice, administration route).

Pitfalls

  • Reversing the compresses — cold for vinca alkaloids or warm for anthracyclines worsens the injury. Anthracycline = cold; vinca = warm.
  • Missing the 6-hour dexrazoxane window, or leaving the cold pack on during the infusion.
  • Flushing the line or applying pressure — both spread the vesicant.
  • Under-reacting to a central-line extravasation because the early signs are hidden.

ICANS (immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity)›
Treatment-relatedHighASTCT 1–4post-2016

Neurotoxicity after CAR-T or T-cell–engaging therapy — tremor, dysgraphia, expressive aphasia, reduced consciousness, seizures; typically follows or overlaps CRS, days after infusion.

Act now
  1. Recognise: new tremor, word-finding difficulty or drowsiness after immune-effector-cell therapy — expressive aphasia is the most specific early sign.
  2. Score the ICE (0–10) and assign the ASTCT grade (see below).
  3. Corticosteroids are first-line — dexamethasone (grade-directed); levetiracetam for seizure prophylaxis/treatment.
  4. Tocilizumab does NOT treat ICANS (no CNS penetration) — use it only for concurrent CRS.

Recognise

Onset usually after CAR-T, often after or during CRS (rarely before), and can recur days later once CRS has settled. Stereotyped progression: tremor, dysgraphia, impaired attention and mild lethargy → expressive aphasia (impaired naming, paraphasic errors, verbal perseveration) → depressed consciousness, and, at the severe end, seizures and cerebral oedema. Headache alone is non-specific and is not ICANS. Exclude other causes of encephalopathy (sedatives, infection, metabolic, intracranial haemorrhage).

Confirm

ICE (Immune effector Cell-associated Encephalopathy) score, /10:

  • Orientation (4) — year, month, city, hospital (1 each).
  • Naming (3) — name 3 objects (e.g. point to clock, pen, button).
  • Following commands (1) — e.g. “show me 2 fingers”, “close your eyes and stick out your tongue”.
  • Writing (1) — write a standard sentence.
  • Attention (1) — count backwards from 100 by 10.

Plus regular assessment of level of consciousness, seizure activity, motor findings, and signs of raised ICP. Neuro obs, fundoscopy for papilloedema; low threshold for MRI brain and EEG (non-convulsive status), and LP where cerebral oedema/raised ICP is not suspected.

Grade — ASTCT 2019

  • G1 ICE 7–9; awakens spontaneously.
  • G2 ICE 3–6; and/or depressed consciousness arousable to voice.
  • G3 ICE 0–2; and/or arousable only to tactile stimulus; any clinical or electrographic seizure that resolves; or focal/local cerebral oedema on imaging.
  • G4 ICE 0 and unarousable (stupor/coma); prolonged (>5 min) or repetitive seizures without return to baseline; deep focal motor weakness (hemi-/paraparesis); or signs of raised ICP / diffuse cerebral oedema (Cushing’s triad, papilloedema, CN VI palsy, decerebrate/decorticate posturing).

The overall grade is set by the most severe domain — a normal ICE does not exclude high-grade ICANS if there is a seizure, raised ICP or deep motor weakness. In children <12 the ICE is replaced by the CAPD (score ≥9 = G3).

Manage (grade-directed)

Corticosteroids are the mainstay — start early; do not wait for a peak. Dosing per ASCO/NCCN algorithms:

  • G1: supportive, close monitoring; consider dexamethasone if not improving, especially with concurrent CRS.
  • G2–3: dexamethasone 10 mg IV q6h (or methylprednisolone equivalent), tapering once improving.
  • G4: high-dose methylprednisolone 1 g/day IV; ICU, airway protection, treat cerebral oedema and status epilepticus.
  • Seizures: levetiracetam for prophylaxis and treatment; benzodiazepine → escalate for status; EEG for non-convulsive seizures.
  • Concurrent CRS: treat that with tocilizumab ± steroids — but tocilizumab does not treat the ICANS itself; it does not reach therapeutic CNS levels. Anakinra is used for steroid-refractory disease in some protocols.

Disposition & escalation

G1–2 → monitored ward/HDU with frequent neuro obs per the cellular-therapy protocol; G3–4 → ICU. Notify the cellular-therapy team and follow the product/institutional pathway. Watch for concurrent CRS and HLH/MAS. Neurology input for seizures, status, or raised ICP.

Pitfalls

  • Giving tocilizumab for ICANS — it does not penetrate the CNS; steroids are first-line.
  • Grading off the ICE alone and missing high-grade features (seizure, raised ICP, deep motor weakness).
  • Attributing new drowsiness to sedatives and delaying steroids.
  • Missing non-convulsive status — get an EEG for unexplained depressed consciousness.

Severe immune-related adverse event crises›
Treatment-relatedHighpost-2016

Grade 3–4 checkpoint-inhibitor toxicities that are immediately life-threatening — myocarditis, colitis with perforation risk, adrenal crisis, pneumonitis, encephalitis, myasthenia/myositis; can present weeks to months after starting, even after stopping.

Act now
  1. Hold the checkpoint inhibitor and think irAE for any new organ dysfunction on/after ICI — the diagnosis is easily missed.
  2. High-dose corticosteroids for grade 3–4 (typically methylprednisolone 1–2 mg/kg/day; 1 g/day IV for myocarditis).
  3. Early organ-specialist input (cardiology, gastroenterology, endocrinology, neurology, ICU).
  4. Steroid-refractory at 48–72 h → second-line immunosuppression (agent depends on the organ).

Recognise

Checkpoint-inhibitor toxicity can hit any organ, on a delay of weeks to months, and sometimes after the drug has stopped. The crises that kill:

  • Myocarditis — rare but the highest case-fatality irAE; new dyspnoea, chest pain, arrhythmia, heart failure. Troponin rise, ECG changes; high mortality in early series.
  • Colitis — high-volume/bloody diarrhoea; risk of toxic megacolon and perforation.
  • Hypophysitis / adrenal crisis — fatigue, headache, hypotension, hyponatraemia, hypoglycaemia; can present as circulatory collapse.
  • Pneumonitis — dyspnoea, cough, hypoxia; can progress to respiratory failure.
  • Neuro / neuromuscular — encephalitis; and a myasthenia–myositis–myocarditis overlap that can cause respiratory failure (monitor FVC/bulbar function).

Confirm

Organ-directed and urgent: myocarditis → troponin, ECG, echo, cardiac MRI (± biopsy); colitis → stool infection screen, CT/flexible sigmoidoscopy; endocrine → cortisol, ACTH, glucose, sodium, thyroid, paired pituitary axis; pneumonitis → CT chest, exclude infection; neuro → MRI, LP, EEG, and CK + repetitive nerve stimulation / anti-AChR for the myasthenia/myositis overlap. Always exclude infection and progression before committing the picture to an irAE.

Manage

Shared principle: hold the ICI, start high-dose corticosteroids, bring in the organ specialist early, and escalate to second-line immunosuppression if steroid-refractory at 48–72 h. Organ-specific:

  • Myocarditis: methylprednisolone 1 g IV daily (pulse), urgent cardiology, telemetry/ICU; steroid-refractory → second-line (e.g. MMF, ATG, abatacept, tofacitinib per emerging data). Permanently discontinue ICI.
  • Colitis: methylprednisolone 1–2 mg/kg/day; refractory or severe → infliximab 5 mg/kg, or vedolizumab 300 mg where infliximab is contraindicated (perforation, sepsis, untreated TB). Surgical review if perforation/megacolon.
  • Hypophysitis / adrenal crisis: stress-dose hydrocortisone first (e.g. 100 mg IV then 50 mg q6h / ~200 mg/24h) and fluids — before any thyroxine, since replacing thyroid hormone ahead of cortisol can precipitate adrenal crisis. High-dose steroids do not reverse the endocrine deficit; hormone replacement is usually lifelong.
  • Pneumonitis: methylprednisolone 1–2 mg/kg/day, O₂, exclude infection; refractory → infliximab / MMF / IVIG.
  • Encephalitis / myasthenia–myositis: high-dose steroids + IVIG or plasma exchange; monitor respiratory function (FVC/NIF) — intubate for neuromuscular respiratory failure. Avoid drugs that worsen myasthenia.

Disposition & escalation

Grade 3–4 irAEs are admissions; myocarditis, neuromuscular respiratory compromise and adrenal crisis are ICU-level. Involve the relevant specialist within hours, not days. Decision to permanently discontinue vs later rechallenge is multidisciplinary — myocarditis and most grade 4 events are permanent discontinuations. Taper steroids slowly (≥4–6 weeks) once controlled.

Pitfalls

  • Giving thyroxine before hydrocortisone in suspected hypopituitarism — can precipitate adrenal crisis. Cortisol first.
  • Infliximab in ICI hepatitis — it is hepatotoxic and contraindicated; use MMF instead.
  • Under-dosing myocarditis — it needs pulse methylprednisolone (1 g), not 1 mg/kg.
  • Anchoring on infection or progression and missing a treatable irAE — and vice versa; exclude infection before high-dose immunosuppression.
  • Missing the myasthenia/myositis overlap until respiratory failure — monitor FVC early.
